Rome to Charlemagne

A timeline through five histories: Mary Beard's SPQR, Chris Wickham's The Inheritance of Rome, John Julius Norwich's A Short History of Byzantium, Tom Holland's In the Shadow of the Sword, and Alessandro Barbero's Charlemagne: Father of a Continent. After Rome's own story below, the timeline stops grouping events by book and groups them by empire instead — Byzantium, the Islamic Caliphate, and the Latin West — since several of these books actually follow the very same empires from different angles. Each card names its source; where two empires cross paths at the same moment, both appear side by side, marked with ⇄.

III. The Mongols — Remaking Byzantium and Islam's World

None of the five books above covers the Mongols either, but their conquests explain two things the timeline above otherwise leaves hanging: why the Seljuk Turks who won at Manzikert in 1071 weren't the ones who eventually took Constantinople, and what actually became of the Abbasid Caliphate after Holland's book ends in 750. Sources linked throughout.

From the steppe to two empires' doorsteps

In 1206, a Mongol chieftain named Temüjin was proclaimed Genghis Khan — ruler of all Mongols — by an assembly of steppe nobility, unifying a patchwork of rival tribes into a single fighting confederation for the first time. Within a few decades his sons and grandsons had built the largest contiguous land empire in history, stretching from Korea to Hungary. Two branches of that expansion collided directly with the empires already on this timeline. See Wikipedia's biography of Genghis Khan and Britannica's overview of the Mongol Empire.

Breaking the Seljuks, making room for the Ottomans

At the Battle of Köse Dağ in 1243, a Mongol army routed the forces of the Sultanate of Rum — the Seljuk Turkish state that had held Anatolia since Manzikert — and reduced it to a vassal. The sultanate never really recovered; it splintered over the following decades into a patchwork of small Turkish principalities, or beyliks. One of those beyliks, based in a small frontier territory near the Byzantine border, was the Ottomans. Their eventual rise to reunite Anatolia and take Constantinople in 1453 is, in a real sense, downstream of this battle: the Mongols broke the power that had beaten Byzantium at Manzikert, and a very different Turkish state filled the vacuum two centuries later. See Wikipedia's account of the Battle of Köse Dağ.

The end of the Abbasid Caliphate

Further south, Genghis Khan's grandson Hulagu led a Mongol army against Baghdad itself. The siege in early 1258 breached the city's walls within days; the Mongols executed the last Abbasid caliph, al-Musta'sim, and killed a large part of the city's population in the sack that followed. It was the effective end of the Abbasid Caliphate that the Islamic Caliphate lane above leaves off with in 750 — the same dynasty that had moved the capital to Baghdad now saw that capital destroyed, five centuries later, by a power from entirely outside the Islamic or Roman worlds. See Wikipedia's account of the Siege of Baghdad.

The Mongols didn't get much further. In 1260, at Ain Jalut in Galilee, the Mamluks of Egypt inflicted the Mongols' first major open-field defeat, stopping their advance into Syria and Egypt for good and setting the western limit of Mongol conquest in the Islamic world. See Britannica's entry on the Battle of Ain Jalut. And in one of history's sharper ironies, the Mongol Ilkhanate that ruled Persia and Iraq after Hulagu converted to Islam itself within a couple of generations — the ruler Ghazan Khan adopted Sunni Islam in 1295 and made it the state religion, so that the dynasty founded by the sack of Baghdad ended up presiding over a Muslim state. See Wikipedia's biography of Ghazan.

IV. Faith and Empire — How the Churches Diverged

None of the five books above is really about the churches themselves, so this section draws on outside sources — linked throughout — to explain why Rome and Constantinople ended up with two different Christianities, what that difference cost each empire, how the rise of Islam then reshaped Byzantine Christianity in particular, and how a third Christian church survived and even reached the Mongol court itself.

A slow theological rift

By the time Charlemagne was crowned in 800, the Christian churches of Rome and Constantinople had been drifting apart for centuries over more than politics. The Western church kept its liturgy in Latin while the Eastern church kept Greek, and doctrine developed along with each language. Rome, on its own authority, added the word Filioque ("and the Son") to the Nicene Creed, asserting that the Holy Spirit proceeds from both the Father and the Son; Constantinople rejected the addition as both theologically wrong and an unauthorized change to a text fixed by ecumenical council. Married clergy, the use of leavened versus unleavened bread in the Eucharist, and fasting rules were smaller but real irritants alongside it — see Britannica's overview of the East–West Schism and Wikipedia's fuller account for the complete list.

The two churches also disagreed about who was in charge. Rome claimed the pope held universal jurisdiction over all Christians as the successor of Saint Peter; Constantinople held that authority was shared among the empire's great patriarchates — Constantinople, Alexandria, Antioch, and Jerusalem, plus Rome itself until 1054 — meeting in council, a model historians call the pentarchy. Mutual excommunications in 1054 made the split official, though it had been building for a long time and many contemporaries didn't treat it as final until the sack of Constantinople in 1204. This summary from Study.com walks through how gradual the process actually was.

Two models of church and state

The deeper difference was structural. In Byzantium, emperor and church were meant to work in what Justinian called a "symphonia" — harmony between imperial and priestly power — but in practice the emperor set most of the terms: he could install or remove the Patriarch of Constantinople and presided over the church councils that settled doctrine. Western writers later called this caesaropapism, though the term appears nowhere in Byzantine sources, and historians now argue it oversimplifies a relationship in which emperors still depended on the church for legitimacy. See Wikipedia's entry on caesaropapism and this Cambridge Core reassessment of the concept.

In the Latin west, the papacy grew into a separate, and eventually rival, center of power. Popes crowned emperors — as Leo III crowned Charlemagne in 800, on the timeline above — which implied the reverse could be argued too: that imperial authority depended on the church's blessing. That argument came to a head in the Investiture Controversy of the late 11th century, when Pope Gregory VII insisted that only the church, not kings or emperors, had the right to appoint bishops. His standoff with the German king Henry IV escalated to excommunication and Henry's famous penance at Canossa, and was only partly resolved by the Concordat of Worms in 1122. See the World History Encyclopedia's account of the Investiture Controversy and Britannica's entry.

How it shaped each empire

Because the Byzantine emperor's authority and the church's were so tightly bound together, a religious dispute could become a state crisis. The Iconoclast Controversy (726–787 and 815–843, already on the timeline above) was exactly that: a fight over whether religious images were idolatry that split the empire for over a century, provoked riots and persecutions, and — because iconoclasm drew more support from the empire's poorer eastern provinces than from Greece and Italy — widened existing regional and social divides. Older scholarship liked to explain it as a direct reaction to Islam: with Arab armies winning battle after battle, the theory went, some Byzantines wondered whether God favored the Muslims' "purer," image-free worship and pushed Emperor Leo III to strip Christianity of its own icons to compete. Most historians today, including Leslie Brubaker and John Haldon, are skeptical of that direct line and point instead to internal theological disputes over idolatry and to an emperor centralizing power amid fiscal and military crisis — even if the shock of near-constant defeat to the new Caliphate still forms the backdrop either way. See Medievalists.net's account of the controversy, Britannica's overview of the Iconoclast era, and Wikipedia's summary of the historiographical debate.

Iconoclasm also had consequences well outside Byzantium. Rome rejected the ban on icons, and the resulting estrangement between the pope and an iconoclast emperor in Constantinople is one reason the papacy started looking west, to the Franks, for military protection instead of east, to an emperor it no longer fully trusted on doctrine. That reorientation runs in a fairly straight line to Pippin's alliance with the papacy in 751 and Charlemagne's coronation in 800 — both already on the parallel timeline above. A dispute over theology, in other words, helped produce a second Roman emperor in the west.

In the west, the church's separateness from royal power cut the other way. Rather than one crisis destabilizing one throne, Latin Christendom ended up with two competing hierarchies — kings and popes — each claiming ultimate authority, which produced centuries of low-grade conflict but also meant no single ruler could fully absorb the church the way Constantinople's emperors effectively could. That balance of power is part of why the papacy outlived every western empire built on top of it, while the fortunes of the Byzantine church rose and fell with the throne in Constantinople itself.

How the rise of Islam remade Byzantine Christianity

The conquests on the Islamic Caliphate lane of the timeline above weren't just a territorial loss for Byzantium — they permanently rearranged Christianity itself. Between 637 and 642, Arab armies took Jerusalem, Antioch, and Alexandria, three of Christianity's five great patriarchates alongside Rome and Constantinople. Almost overnight, Constantinople became the only patriarchate still governed by a state that called itself Roman; Rome was independent of the empire anyway, and the other three now answered to a Muslim caliph. The old model of five co-equal patriarchates governing the church together — the pentarchy — kept its name and its ceremonial weight but lost its practical substance, leaving Constantinople with a claim to eastern Christian leadership it never had to share again. See CNEWA's overview of the pentarchy and Wikipedia's entry on the concept.

The same conquests also resolved, by removing the argument's home ground, one of the empire's oldest internal fights. Egypt and much of Syria had been strongholds of Miaphysite Christians, who rejected the Chalcedonian formula on the nature of Christ that Constantinople insisted on — a split going back to the Council of Chalcedon in 451 that emperors had spent a century failing to paper over. Once those provinces were under Muslim rule, contact between Chalcedonian and Miaphysite Christians was largely cut off, and the dispute inside the empire's remaining territory cooled: Constantinople reaffirmed strict Chalcedonian orthodoxy at the Third Council of Constantinople in 680–681, no longer needing to bargain with a large dissenting population next door. Byzantium came out of it more theologically uniform, but only because the Christians who disagreed with it — the ancestors of today's Coptic and Syriac Orthodox churches — had been permanently split off into a separate world under Islamic rule. See Wikipedia's account of the Chalcedonian schism and Britannica's entry on the Copts.

Islam also became something Byzantine Christians had to think about theologically, not just militarily. John of Damascus, a Christian who served in the Umayyad civil administration in Damascus before becoming a monk, wrote one of the first sustained Christian responses to Islam around the 730s — and he classified it not as a new religion but as a Christian heresy, "the Heresy of the Ishmaelites," to be refuted the way earlier heresies had been. That framing shaped Christian polemic against Islam for centuries afterward. See Sidney Griffith's study of John of Damascus and Islam in the journal Hugoye.

A third Christianity: the Church of the East

Chalcedonian versus Miaphysite wasn't the only split in eastern Christianity — it wasn't even the oldest. The Church of the East had broken away earlier still, over the Council of Ephesus in 431 rather than Chalcedon in 451, in a dispute that centered on Nestorius, patriarch of Constantinople, and how starkly Christ's human and divine natures should be described. Crucially, this church was based not in Roman territory but in Sasanian Persia, whose emperors — happy to patronize a church Constantinople considered heretical — gave it protection precisely because it had no loyalty to Byzantium. It never had to survive a Byzantine crackdown, because it was never Byzantium's to crack down on. See Wikipedia's entry on the Church of the East and Wikipedia's account of the Nestorian schism.

Being outside the Roman world let it go further than any other branch of Christianity. Missionaries and merchants carried it east along the Silk Road into Central Asia and India, and by the 7th century into Tang China, where a stone stele erected in 781 records an established Christian community at the imperial court. Later, under Islamic rule, the Church of the East fared comparatively well as a protected minority — the same conquests that permanently split off the Copts and Syriac Orthodox from Constantinople left the Church of the East more or less where it already was, outside the empire, going about business its rivals in Byzantium had never controlled either.

Its most striking moment came centuries later, at the Mongol court. Sorghaghtani Beki, a Church of the East Christian from the Kerait tribe, married into the family of Genghis Khan and became mother to four sons who, between them, ruled most of Eurasia: Möngke and Kublai as Great Khans, Hulagu as founder of the Ilkhanate, and Ariq Böke as a rival claimant to the throne. Hulagu's own chief wife, Doquz Khatun, was Christian too, and his sack of Baghdad in 1258 — recounted in the Mongol section above — was cheered on by some Christian chroniclers as revenge on a Muslim power that had long persecuted them. That moment of Christian influence at the summit of the Mongol world didn't last: within a generation the Ilkhanate itself turned to Islam under Ghazan Khan, and Tamerlane's campaigns in the following century devastated the Church of the East's communities across Central Asia and Persia, reducing a church that had once stretched from Iraq to China to the much smaller Assyrian Church of the East that survives today. See Wikipedia's entry on Sorghaghtani Beki.
