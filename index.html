Rome to Charlemagne

A timeline through four histories: Mary Beard's SPQR, Chris Wickham's The Inheritance of Rome (which follows both the Franks in the west and Byzantium in the east across the same centuries), John Julius Norwich's A Short History of Byzantium, and Alessandro Barbero's Charlemagne: Father of a Continent — from the founding of Rome to the making of medieval Europe.

SPQR (753 BC – 212 AD) The Inheritance of Rome (c. 300 – 1000 AD) A Short History of Byzantium (330 – 1453 AD) Charlemagne (742 – 814 AD)

I. SPQR — The Rise and Rule of Rome

Mary Beard's history of Rome from its legendary founding to the extension of citizenship across the empire.

753 BC

Legendary Founding of Rome

Romulus is said to found the city on the Palatine Hill, beginning the traditional Roman calendar (AUC).

509 BC

The Republic Founded

The last king, Tarquin the Proud, is expelled. Rome becomes a republic governed by elected magistrates and the Senate.

c. 494 BC

Conflict of the Orders Begins

Plebeians secede from Rome in protest, winning the office of tribune — the long struggle between patricians and plebeians for political power begins.

390 BC

The Gauls Sack Rome

A Gallic force under Brennus defeats Rome and occupies the city, a trauma that lingers in Roman memory for centuries.

264–146 BC

The Punic Wars

Three wars against Carthage, including Hannibal's invasion of Italy, end with Rome's total destruction of Carthage and dominance of the western Mediterranean.

133–121 BC

The Gracchi Reforms

Tiberius and Gaius Gracchus push land and political reforms and are killed for it — the Republic's political violence begins in earnest.

88–82 BC

Sulla's Civil War and Dictatorship

Sulla marches on Rome, then rules as dictator, proscribing enemies — a preview of one-man rule to come.

60 BC

The First Triumvirate

Julius Caesar, Pompey, and Crassus form an informal alliance to dominate Roman politics.

49 BC

Caesar Crosses the Rubicon

Caesar leads his army into Italy in defiance of the Senate, igniting civil war.

44 BC

Assassination of Julius Caesar

Caesar is stabbed to death by senators on the Ides of March, having been declared dictator for life.

31 BC

Battle of Actium

Octavian defeats Mark Antony and Cleopatra, leaving him sole master of the Roman world.

27 BC

Augustus and the Birth of the Empire

Octavian takes the name Augustus and, while preserving republican forms, becomes Rome's first emperor.

AD 14

Death of Augustus

Tiberius succeeds him, confirming that imperial power can pass by inheritance — the principate becomes a monarchy in fact.

AD 64

The Great Fire of Rome

Fire devastates the city under Nero, who blames the Christians and begins their persecution.

AD 79

Eruption of Vesuvius

Pompeii and Herculaneum are buried in ash, preserving an extraordinary snapshot of everyday Roman life.

AD 117

The Empire at Its Greatest Extent

Under Trajan, Roman territory reaches its widest span, from Britain to Mesopotamia.

AD 212

The Antonine Constitution

Emperor Caracalla grants Roman citizenship to nearly all free inhabitants of the empire — the point where Mary Beard's SPQR closes its narrative.

II. The Inheritance of Rome — Europe After the Fall

Chris Wickham's account of how Rome's collapse in the west reshaped Europe and the Mediterranean world, c. 400–1000 AD — tracking the Franks and other western successor kingdoms alongside the surviving Roman state in the east, Byzantium, across the same centuries.

AD 312

Constantine's Conversion

Constantine wins the Battle of the Milvian Bridge and begins the empire's turn toward Christianity.

AD 330

Founding of Constantinople

Constantine dedicates his new capital in the east, shifting the empire's center of gravity and setting up centuries of divergence between Latin west and Greek east.

AD 376–410

Gothic Migration and the Sack of Rome

Pressed by the Huns, Gothic groups cross into the empire; in 410 Alaric's Visigoths sack Rome itself, a profound shock to the Roman world.

AD 451

Battle of the Catalaunian Plains

A Roman-Gothic coalition halts Attila the Hun's advance into Gaul.

AD 476

Deposition of Romulus Augustulus

The Germanic general Odoacer deposes the last western Roman emperor. Wickham treats this as a marker rather than a sudden rupture — Roman institutions and culture persist for generations.

AD 493

Theoderic's Ostrogothic Kingdom in Italy

Theoderic the Great establishes a Gothic kingdom that consciously preserves Roman administration and culture.

AD 527–565

Justinian's Reign and Reconquest

The eastern emperor Justinian codifies Roman law and attempts to reconquer the west, including Italy and North Africa, at devastating cost.

AD 568

Lombard Invasion of Italy

The Lombards enter Italy and establish a kingdom, fragmenting the peninsula for centuries.

AD 622

The Hijra and Rise of Islam

Muhammad's migration to Medina marks the start of the Islamic calendar; within decades Arab armies overrun the Byzantine and Sasanian east.

AD 632–711

Islamic Expansion Across the Mediterranean

Arab conquests sweep across the Levant, North Africa, and finally Visigothic Spain, transforming the political map Rome once unified.

AD 664

Synod of Whitby

The Northumbrian church adopts Roman rather than Celtic Christian practice, tying Anglo-Saxon England more closely to continental Christendom.

AD 711–718

Fall of Visigothic Spain

Umayyad forces conquer most of the Iberian peninsula, ending Visigothic rule and beginning centuries of al-Andalus.

AD 751

Pippin the Short Becomes King

The Carolingian mayor of the palace deposes the last Merovingian king with papal sanction, founding the Carolingian dynasty.

AD 800

Coronation of Charlemagne

Pope Leo III crowns Charlemagne emperor in Rome — a symbolic revival of the western empire, and the moment where this history overlaps with Barbero's biography below.

AD 843

Treaty of Verdun

Charlemagne's grandsons divide the Carolingian Empire into three kingdoms, laying the ground for the future shapes of France and Germany.

AD 962

Otto I Crowned Holy Roman Emperor

The German king Otto I is crowned emperor in Rome, founding the line that becomes the Holy Roman Empire.

c. AD 1000

A New Political Landscape

By the year 1000, Wickham's narrative closes on a Europe of feudal lordships, emerging kingdoms, and a Latin Christendom distinct from both Byzantium and the Islamic world.

III. A Short History of Byzantium — The Eastern Empire

John Julius Norwich's account of the Roman state that never fell in 476 — the Greek-speaking, Christian empire ruled from Constantinople until 1453.

AD 330

Constantinople Dedicated

Constantine dedicates his "New Rome" on the site of Byzantium — the city that gives this empire its modern name and will serve as its capital for over a thousand years.

AD 395

Permanent Division of the Empire

On the death of Theodosius I, the empire splits between his two sons and is never reunited — the eastern half survives while the western half collapses within a century.

AD 532

The Nika Riots

Chariot-racing factions nearly topple Justinian in a week of rioting that burns much of Constantinople; Justinian, urged on by Empress Theodora, crushes the revolt and rebuilds the city.

AD 537

Hagia Sophia Completed

Justinian's great domed cathedral is dedicated, an architectural statement of imperial and Christian ambition that stands for centuries as the largest church in the world.

AD 626

Constantinople Withstands Siege

A combined Avar and Persian siege fails to take the capital, preserving the empire at one of its lowest points.

AD 636–698

Loss of the Eastern and African Provinces

Arab armies strip Byzantium of Syria, Palestine, Egypt, and eventually North Africa, permanently shrinking the empire to Anatolia and the Balkans.

AD 674–678 & 717–718

The Great Arab Sieges of Constantinople

Twice the Umayyad Caliphate besieges the capital by land and sea; twice it is repelled, aided by the secret incendiary weapon known as Greek fire.

AD 730–787 & 815–843

The Iconoclast Controversy

Two long periods of imperial policy ban the veneration of religious images, convulsing Byzantine religious and political life before icon veneration is permanently restored in 843.

AD 863

Mission of Cyril and Methodius

Byzantine missionary brothers are sent to convert the Slavs, devising the script that becomes Cyrillic and spreading Orthodox Christianity across Eastern Europe.

AD 976–1025

Basil II "the Bulgar-Slayer"

Byzantium reaches its medieval military peak under Basil II, who crushes the Bulgarian Empire after decades of campaigning, restoring imperial power in the Balkans.

AD 1054

The Great Schism

Mutual excommunications between Rome and Constantinople formalize the lasting split between the Catholic and Orthodox churches.

AD 1071

Battle of Manzikert

Seljuk Turks crush a Byzantine army and capture the emperor, opening Anatolia — the empire's heartland — to permanent Turkish settlement.

AD 1204

The Fourth Crusade Sacks Constantinople

Crusader and Venetian forces, diverted from their intended target, storm and pillage Constantinople itself, installing a Latin Empire and shattering Byzantine power beyond full recovery.

AD 1261

Byzantine Restoration

Michael VIII Palaiologos retakes Constantinople from the Latins, but the restored empire is a fraction of its former self, beset by rivals on every side.

AD 1453

Fall of Constantinople

Ottoman Sultan Mehmed II takes the city after a weeks-long siege, killing the last emperor, Constantine XI, and ending the Roman state founded over two thousand years earlier — the close of Norwich's history.

IV. Charlemagne — Father of a Continent

Alessandro Barbero's biography of the Frankish king who briefly reunified much of western Europe under a single crown, 742–814 AD.

c. 742

Birth of Charlemagne

Charles is born to Pippin the Short, grandson of the great Frankish leader Charles Martel.

768

Charlemagne Becomes King of the Franks

On Pippin's death, the kingdom is divided between Charlemagne and his brother Carloman.

771

Sole Rule

Carloman dies and Charlemagne becomes sole king of the Franks.

772–804

The Saxon Wars

A brutal, decades-long campaign subdues and forcibly Christianizes the Saxons, including the mass execution at Verden in 782.

774

Conquest of the Lombard Kingdom

Charlemagne defeats the Lombards in Italy and takes their crown, becoming King of the Lombards as well as the Franks.

778

Battle of Roncevaux Pass

Charlemagne's rearguard is ambushed and destroyed in the Pyrenees during a retreat from Spain — later immortalized in the legend of Roland.

c. 780s–790s

The Carolingian Renaissance

Charlemagne gathers scholars such as Alcuin of York to his court at Aachen, sparking a revival of learning, manuscript copying, and educational reform across the realm.

791–796

Campaigns Against the Avars

Frankish forces crush the Avar khaganate in the Danube basin, seizing enormous treasure and extending Carolingian power east.

800

Crowned Emperor in Rome

On Christmas Day, Pope Leo III crowns Charlemagne Emperor of the Romans, reviving the imperial title in the west for the first time since 476.

802

Reform of Law and Government

Charlemagne issues capitularies standardizing law and dispatches missi dominici — royal envoys — to inspect and enforce his rule across the empire.

813

Louis the Pious Crowned Co-Emperor

Charlemagne, anticipating his death, crowns his son Louis as co-emperor to secure the succession.

814

Death of Charlemagne

Charlemagne dies at his palace in Aachen and is buried there. His empire outlives him by only a generation before it begins to fracture — but his reign leaves a lasting template for European kingship and culture.
