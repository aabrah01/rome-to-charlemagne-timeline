Rome to Charlemagne

A timeline through five histories: Mary Beard's SPQR, Chris Wickham's The Inheritance of Rome (which follows both the Franks in the west and Byzantium in the east across the same centuries), John Julius Norwich's A Short History of Byzantium, Tom Holland's In the Shadow of the Sword (which follows Rome and Persia's collapse alongside the rise of Islam across those very same decades), and Alessandro Barbero's Charlemagne: Father of a Continent. Where two or more books describe the same moment, their entries sit side by side at the same point in time, in separate lanes.

I. SPQR — The Rise and Rule of Rome

Mary Beard's history of Rome from its legendary founding to the extension of citizenship across the empire. No other book in this set covers this period, so it runs as a single track.

753 BC

Legendary Founding of Rome

Romulus is said to found the city on the Palatine Hill, beginning the traditional Roman calendar (AUC).

509 BC

The Republic Founded

The last king, Tarquin the Proud, is expelled. Rome becomes a republic governed by elected magistrates and the Senate.

c. 494 BC

Conflict of the Orders Begins

Plebeians secede from Rome in protest, winning the office of tribune — the long struggle between patricians and plebeians for political power begins.

390 BC

The Gauls Sack Rome

A Gallic force under Brennus defeats Rome and occupies the city, a trauma that lingers in Roman memory for centuries.

264–146 BC

The Punic Wars

Three wars against Carthage, including Hannibal's invasion of Italy, end with Rome's total destruction of Carthage and dominance of the western Mediterranean.

133–121 BC

The Gracchi Reforms

Tiberius and Gaius Gracchus push land and political reforms and are killed for it — the Republic's political violence begins in earnest.

88–82 BC

Sulla's Civil War and Dictatorship

Sulla marches on Rome, then rules as dictator, proscribing enemies — a preview of one-man rule to come.

60 BC

The First Triumvirate

Julius Caesar, Pompey, and Crassus form an informal alliance to dominate Roman politics.

49 BC

Caesar Crosses the Rubicon

Caesar leads his army into Italy in defiance of the Senate, igniting civil war.

44 BC

Assassination of Julius Caesar

Caesar is stabbed to death by senators on the Ides of March, having been declared dictator for life.

31 BC

Battle of Actium

Octavian defeats Mark Antony and Cleopatra, leaving him sole master of the Roman world.

27 BC

Augustus and the Birth of the Empire

Octavian takes the name Augustus and, while preserving republican forms, becomes Rome's first emperor.

AD 14

Death of Augustus

Tiberius succeeds him, confirming that imperial power can pass by inheritance — the principate becomes a monarchy in fact.

AD 64

The Great Fire of Rome

Fire devastates the city under Nero, who blames the Christians and begins their persecution.

AD 79

Eruption of Vesuvius

Pompeii and Herculaneum are buried in ash, preserving an extraordinary snapshot of everyday Roman life.

AD 117

The Empire at Its Greatest Extent

Under Trajan, Roman territory reaches its widest span, from Britain to Mesopotamia.

AD 212

The Antonine Constitution

Emperor Caracalla grants Roman citizenship to nearly all free inhabitants of the empire — the point where Mary Beard's SPQR closes its narrative.

II. Parallel Histories — Rome's Successors, Byzantium, the Rise of Islam, and Charlemagne

From c. 300 AD, four books cover overlapping ground from different vantage points: Wickham's Inheritance of Rome tracks both the Frankish west and Byzantine east, Norwich's Short History of Byzantium stays fixed on Constantinople through 1453, Tom Holland's In the Shadow of the Sword follows Rome and Persia's collapse into the rise of Islam (Holland takes a more source-skeptical view of the traditional narrative than most standard accounts, and prefers "Believers" to "Muslims" for the earliest generation), and Barbero's Charlemagne zooms into one Frankish reign, 742–814. III. Faith and Empire — How the Churches Diverged

None of the five books above is really about the churches themselves, so this section draws on outside sources — linked throughout — to explain why Rome and Constantinople ended up with two different Christianities, and what that difference cost each empire.

A slow theological rift

By the time Charlemagne was crowned in 800, the Christian churches of Rome and Constantinople had been drifting apart for centuries over more than politics. The Western church kept its liturgy in Latin while the Eastern church kept Greek, and doctrine developed along with each language. Rome, on its own authority, added the word Filioque ("and the Son") to the Nicene Creed, asserting that the Holy Spirit proceeds from both the Father and the Son; Constantinople rejected the addition as both theologically wrong and an unauthorized change to a text fixed by ecumenical council. Married clergy, the use of leavened versus unleavened bread in the Eucharist, and fasting rules were smaller but real irritants alongside it — see Britannica's overview of the East–West Schism and Wikipedia's fuller account for the complete list.

The two churches also disagreed about who was in charge. Rome claimed the pope held universal jurisdiction over all Christians as the successor of Saint Peter; Constantinople held that authority was shared among the empire's great patriarchates — Constantinople, Alexandria, Antioch, and Jerusalem, plus Rome itself until 1054 — meeting in council, a model historians call the pentarchy. Mutual excommunications in 1054 made the split official, though it had been building for a long time and many contemporaries didn't treat it as final until the sack of Constantinople in 1204. This summary from Study.com walks through how gradual the process actually was.

Two models of church and state

The deeper difference was structural. In Byzantium, emperor and church were meant to work in what Justinian called a "symphonia" — harmony between imperial and priestly power — but in practice the emperor set most of the terms: he could install or remove the Patriarch of Constantinople and presided over the church councils that settled doctrine. Western writers later called this caesaropapism, though the term appears nowhere in Byzantine sources, and historians now argue it oversimplifies a relationship in which emperors still depended on the church for legitimacy. See Wikipedia's entry on caesaropapism and this Cambridge Core reassessment of the concept.

In the Latin west, the papacy grew into a separate, and eventually rival, center of power. Popes crowned emperors — as Leo III crowned Charlemagne in 800, on the timeline above — which implied the reverse could be argued too: that imperial authority depended on the church's blessing. That argument came to a head in the Investiture Controversy of the late 11th century, when Pope Gregory VII insisted that only the church, not kings or emperors, had the right to appoint bishops. His standoff with the German king Henry IV escalated to excommunication and Henry's famous penance at Canossa, and was only partly resolved by the Concordat of Worms in 1122. See the World History Encyclopedia's account of the Investiture Controversy and Britannica's entry.

How it shaped each empire

Because the Byzantine emperor's authority and the church's were so tightly bound together, a religious dispute could become a state crisis. The Iconoclast Controversy (726–787 and 815–843, already on the timeline above) was exactly that: a fight over whether religious images were idolatry that split the empire for over a century, provoked riots and persecutions, and — because iconoclasm drew more support from the empire's poorer eastern provinces than from Greece and Italy — widened existing regional and social divides. See Medievalists.net's account of the controversy and Britannica's overview of the Iconoclast era.

Iconoclasm also had consequences well outside Byzantium. Rome rejected the ban on icons, and the resulting estrangement between the pope and an iconoclast emperor in Constantinople is one reason the papacy started looking west, to the Franks, for military protection instead of east, to an emperor it no longer fully trusted on doctrine. That reorientation runs in a fairly straight line to Pippin's alliance with the papacy in 751 and Charlemagne's coronation in 800 — both already on the parallel timeline above. A dispute over theology, in other words, helped produce a second Roman emperor in the west.

In the west, the church's separateness from royal power cut the other way. Rather than one crisis destabilizing one throne, Latin Christendom ended up with two competing hierarchies — kings and popes — each claiming ultimate authority, which produced centuries of low-grade conflict but also meant no single ruler could fully absorb the church the way Constantinople's emperors effectively could. That balance of power is part of why the papacy outlived every western empire built on top of it, while the fortunes of the Byzantine church rose and fell with the throne in Constantinople itself.
